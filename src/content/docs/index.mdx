	<section class="vamp-problem vamp-panel" aria-labelledby="vamp-problem-heading">
		<div class="vamp-problem-copy">
			<p class="vamp-label">Why Change Email</p>
			<h2 id="vamp-problem-heading">Modern email is still organized around weak identity and after-the-fact defense.</h2>
			<p>
				The current system asks receivers to infer trust from headers, heuristics, and reputation
				after a message is already in motion. By that point, the protocol has already handed the
				receiver the cost and the risk.
			</p>
			<ul>
				<li>Email addresses are weak identity claims, not cryptographic principals tied to a real actor.</li>
				<li>Message handling is opaque; recipients rarely get a trustworthy chain of custody from origin to inbox.</li>
				<li>Attackers can send unsolicited bulk mail at near-zero marginal cost.</li>
				<li>Operators spend billions each year filtering abuse at industrial scale just to keep inboxes usable.</li>
				<li>Users are still expected to inspect headers, wording, and context manually to spot fraud.</li>
			</ul>
			<p>
				VAMP starts from a different premise: identity should be verifiable before or during delivery,
				provenance should be explicit, and unauthorized scale should impose cost on the sender instead
				of being dumped on the receiver.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="vamp-problem-list" aria-label="Core problems VAMP is designed to change">
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>Weak sender identity</strong>
				<span>Legacy email makes sender identity easy to spoof. Display names, headers, and even whole message paths can be manipulated to make malicious mail look legitimate.</span>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>No chain of custody</strong>
				<span>Recipients rarely get a trustworthy record of who originated a message, which systems handled it, what changed in transit, or whether it was redistributed by an authorized service.</span>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>Abuse is cheap</strong>
				<span>Spam, phishing, and mass solicitation are cheap to send at scale. The protocol gives attackers near-zero marginal cost while letting them externalize the damage.</span>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>Filtering is expensive</strong>
				<span>Receivers pay the bill for a broken trust model: filtering, storage, machine learning, user training, incident response, and fraud recovery all happen after the message is sent.</span>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>Consent is weak and sender-controlled</strong>
				<span>Opt-in, subscription state, and unsubscribe handling are usually maintained by the sender. Receivers can verify that a sender supports these workflows, but not that the recipient actually consented.</span>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-problem-item">
				<strong>Trust is evaluated too late</strong>
				<span>Legacy email usually discovers security, capability, and recipient validity after the message is already in motion. By then, the receiver is left to sort out what should have been rejected earlier.</span>
			</article>
		</div>
	</section>

	<section class="vamp-principles" aria-labelledby="vamp-principles-heading">
		<div class="vamp-section-head">
			<p class="vamp-label">Identity-First Mail</p>
			<h2 id="vamp-principles-heading">VAMP changes the default story of a message.</h2>
			<p>
				This is not just a better spam filter. VAMP is a different substrate for email-shaped
				messaging: domain-authoritative identity, explicit custody, secure native transport, and
				sender accountability built into the protocol instead of layered on top of SMTP.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="vamp-principles-grid">
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">01</p>
				<h3>Domain-authoritative identity</h3>
				<p>
					The namespace owner is the identity authority. Trust begins at a domain trust anchor, not
					at a string in a `From` field.
				</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">02</p>
				<h3>Encrypt to the user, sign with the device</h3>
				<p>
					Messages target the user for asynchronous, multi-device mail, while device signatures make
					origin-specific trust visible.
				</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">03</p>
				<h3>Make custody explicit</h3>
				<p>
					A message is not just payload plus signature. It is a cryptographically verifiable record
					of who originated it and which meaningful actors handled it.
				</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">04</p>
				<h3>Attach policy to the real edge</h3>
				<p>
					Native delivery runs from sender-edge to receiver-edge, giving the protocol a clean place
					to attach identity, policy, downgrade rules, and sender-side cost.
				</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">05</p>
				<h3>Make unauthorized scale expensive</h3>
				<p>
					Unauthorized bulk delivery should impose sender-side marginal cost instead of dumping the
					burden on receivers, filters, and incident response teams.
				</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-panel vamp-principle">
				<p class="vamp-index">06</p>
				<h3>Prove consent for redistribution</h3>
				<p>
					Mailing lists, newsletters, and notifications should carry verifiable membership or
					subscription proof instead of relying on sender-maintained opt-in claims.
				</p>
			</article>
		</div>
	</section>

	<section class="vamp-architecture vamp-panel" aria-labelledby="vamp-architecture-heading">
		<div class="vamp-architecture-copy">
			<p class="vamp-label">Trust Model</p>
			<h2 id="vamp-architecture-heading">Trust flows from device to user to organization to domain.</h2>
			<p>
				VAMP is fundamentally domain-authoritative. Devices hold the signing keys, users remain the
				stable messaging principals, organizations manage enrollment and revocation, and domains act
				as the trust anchors for the namespace. Humans never touch
				the crypto.
			</p>
			<div class="vamp-key-points">
				<p class="vamp-key-point"><strong>Encryption:</strong> target the user identity, not each device.</p>
				<p class="vamp-key-point"><strong>Signing:</strong> device-level origin remains visible and policy-relevant.</p>
				<p class="vamp-key-point"><strong>Transport:</strong> sender-edge adds organizational attestation for external delivery.</p>
			</div>
		</div>
		<div class="vamp-chain" aria-label="VAMP trust chain">
			<article class="vamp-chain-step">
				<p class="vamp-doc-label">Device</p>
				<h3>Cryptographic origin</h3>
				<p>Hardware-bound signing keys establish where user actions actually came from.</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-chain-step">
				<p class="vamp-doc-label">User</p>
				<h3>Stable messaging principal</h3>
				<p>The identity recipients reason about and the identity to which messages are encrypted.</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-chain-step">
				<p class="vamp-doc-label">Organization</p>
				<h3>Administrative authority</h3>
				<p>Enrolls devices, authorizes outbound transport, and revokes compromised state.</p>
			</article>
			<article class="vamp-chain-step">
				<p class="vamp-doc-label">Domain</p>
				<h3>Trust anchor for the namespace</h3>
				<p>Defines which authority is actually responsible for identities under that domain.</p>
			</article>
		</div>
	</section>

	<section class="vamp-custody vamp-panel" aria-labelledby="vamp-custody-heading">
		<div class="vamp-section-head">
			<p class="vamp-label">Chain Of Custody</p>
			<h2 id="vamp-custody-heading">Every message already has a provenance story.</h2>
			<p>
				In VAMP, chain of custody is not a special mechanism reserved for mailing lists or
				forwarding. It is the normal shape of a message. That lets recipients distinguish authorship,
				redistribution, service-generated delivery, and explicit handling without relying on loose
				header conventions.
			</p>
		</div>
